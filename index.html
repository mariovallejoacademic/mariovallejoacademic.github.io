About me

I am Mario Alberto Vallejo Reyes, a Research Assistant in the Audio Lab at the University of York (UK). I design controlled listening tests, manage data collection and analysis, develop audio signal-processing workflows, and co-author peer-reviewed publications.

I hold an MSc in Audio and Music Technology (University of York), an MSc in Educational Technology (Universidad Da Vinci), and a BSc in Audio Engineering (SAE Institute Mexico). My research investigates psychoacoustic phenomena—recent work includes a validation study of soft-clipping effects (AES Latin American Convention 2024) and a MIDI-based analysis of Mexican folk vocal melodies (arXiv:2503.24243).

Over the past decade I have engineered recordings in professional studios, contributed to a Latin Grammy–nominated project, and mixed Dolby Atmos tracks featured on Apple Music. My credits appear on productions with almost two billion streams, and my VR-based interactive music system represented York at Game Republic 2024. I am Avid-certified in Pro Tools (levels 101 and 110), hold a Producer Certificate in Ableton Live, and am Waves Audio certified.

Abbey Road Talk — Studio Three

Distinctions • Abbey Road Studios, Studio Three — London • August 22, 2025

I was honoured to be invited to present in Studio Three at Abbey Road Studios during the Audiomovers Educators Advisory Group—a day of talks and demos for audio educators from the UK, US, and beyond. My session shared a University of York case study on teaching analog mixing via on-site streaming: run a focused console session in the studio while a full cohort listens in high quality from a lecture theatre, with questions handled live by a moderator so the mixer’s flow remains uninterrupted.

What I presented. A reliable, class-ready signal path (console → converters → Audiomovers ListenTo for lossless streaming), live video (DAW and camera feeds switchable via a small MIDI controller), and a monitoring/talkback setup that keeps the engineer in the zone while the room follows every move. We discussed redundancy, latency, and repeatability—what works week after week, not just once.

Why it matters. One session scales to a full cohort instead of seven repeats. Students witness a real, unbroken mix from start to finish and still get meaningful interaction. The same pattern translates to recording technique, production workflow, and spatial-audio demonstrations.
